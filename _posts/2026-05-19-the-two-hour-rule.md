I have a rule for any problem that isn't moving: **two hours of honest effort, then I tell someone.**

## Not too early, not too late

Asking for help in the first ten minutes robs you of the struggle that actually teaches. But pride keeps people stuck for *days* on things a colleague could unblock in five minutes. Two hours is my compromise — long enough to have really tried, short enough that I haven't burned a day.

## What "honest effort" means

The clock only counts if I'm actually working the problem:

- I've read the relevant code and docs, not just skimmed
- I've written down what I expected vs. what happened
- I have at least one specific question, not "it doesn't work"

That last point is the secret. The act of writing a *specific* question solves the problem outright more often than I'd like to admit. Sometimes I never send the message.

## The social half

The rule only works if asking is safe. On teams where questions are treated as gaps in competence, people hide and the two-hour rule quietly becomes the two-week rule. If you lead a team, the most useful thing you can do is ask dumb questions in public yourself.
